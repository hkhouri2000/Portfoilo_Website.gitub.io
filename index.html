About Me

As a current student pursuing a double major in computer science and mathematics, I am highly interested in three specific areas: artificial intelligence (AI), quantum computing, and game theories. My skills and academic background in computer science and mathematics have prepared me to excel in these areas and make meaningful contributions to the field.

Firstly, AI is a fascinating field that has captured my attention and imagination. The potential for AI to revolutionize various industries, such as healthcare, transportation, education, and entertainment, makes it an exciting and dynamic field to be a part of. My academic background in computer science and mathematics has provided me with a strong foundation in key concepts such as machine learning, natural language processing, and computer vision. Through hands-on experience, such as implementing a spam filter using naïve Bayes classification and investigating the use of data science and AI in wildlife tracking to combat elephant poaching, I have gained a deeper understanding of these concepts. My passion for AI, coupled with my academic background, makes me eager to contribute to the field and be a part of the larger picture.

Secondly, quantum computing is an area of interest to me due to its potential to solve problems that are beyond the capabilities of classical computers. My coursework in quantum computation has given me a strong understanding of the basics of quantum mechanics and how they can be applied to computation. I have also had the opportunity to work with quantum simulation software, which allows me to experiment with simple quantum algorithms and see how they behave on a quantum computer. This has given me a deeper understanding of the potential and limitations of quantum computation and has sparked my curiosity to learn more about this exciting field.

Finally, the game of theories is another area of interest to me within the mathematics major. The game of theories is a framework for understanding and comparing the relative strengths and limitations of different mathematical models or theories. It has applications in fields such as economics, physics, and computer science and can be used to study a wide range of phenomena, from the evolution of species to the behavior of markets. Through my coursework in the game of theories, I have gained a strong understanding of key concepts such as the minimax theorem, principle of induction, and the Nash equilibrium. These concepts have helped me to better understand how different theories can be applied to real-world situations and how they can be compared and evaluated based on their predictive power and other factors. I am particularly interested in using the game of theories to study the behavior of complex systems, such as social networks or economic markets, and to understand how different factors can influence the outcomes of these systems.

In summary, my passion for AI, quantum computing, and game theories, coupled with my academic background in computer science and mathematics, make me an excellent fit for the BS in Artificial Intelligence and Mathematics program. I am excited to continue exploring and learning in these fields and believe that my skills and interests make me a strong candidate for this program.

    Field-Programming Gate Array (FPGA) MIPS Five Stage Pipelined CPU

      In this project, I implemented a pipelined MIPS processor datapath with various features. The project consisted of two main parts:

      Part 1: Implemented a 5-stage pipeline (FDEMW) with branch resolution in the Decode (D) stage and non-memory hazard detection in the Decode (D) stage. Additionally, a 2-cycle pipelined multiplication unit spanning the Execute (E) and Memory (M) stages was included, which reads inputs in the Execute stage and produces outputs in the Memory stage (available in the Writeback stage). The project did not include a hardware divider. For Part 1, I was responsible for implementing hazard detection logic for all hazards and forwarding logic for the EX stage for some hazards, as well as forwarding logic for the M stage for all hazards.

      Part 2: Implemented branch prediction extensions, including a 2K-entry bimodal table of 2-bit predictors with an initial state of "10." Additionally, a 32-entry Branch Target Buffer (BTB) was implemented to cache the contents (not the address) of supported branch and jump instructions (BEQ, BNE, J, JAL). Instructions like JR and JALR were explicitly not cached due to target indirection.

      For both parts, I implemented an instruction cache and a data cache as replacements for the Instruction Memory (IMEM) and Data Memory (DMEM), respectively, in the memory hierarchy. The instruction cache, a 2KB direct-mapped cache with a block size of 64 bytes, served cache misses from the IMEM. The data cache, a 2KB 2-way associative cache with an inverse-MRU eviction policy, was used to serve cache misses from the DMEM. The DMEM had a write-back policy and stall-allocated on store misses. Evicted dirty lines were written back to the DMEM.

      Throughout the project, I worked with a provided framework that included files for hazard detection, forwarding logic, the branch history table (BHT), and the implementation of the caches. The project was divided into milestones, and for Milestone 1, I completed parts 1 and 2, utilizing a dummy memory hierarchy to test the design before integrating the caches.

      The framework files relevant to my edits and additions for parts 1 and 2 were:

      HazardDetection.v: Implementation of hazard detection logic. EXForwarding.v: Implementation of forwarding logic for the Execute (EX) stage. MEMforwarding.v: Implementation of forwarding logic for the Memory (MEM) stage. BHT.v: Implementation of the branch history table for branch prediction. Overall, this project allowed me to gain experience in designing and implementing a pipelined MIPS processor datapath with various pipeline stages, hazard detection, forwarding logic, branch prediction, and memory hierarchy using instruction and data caches.

    Room Scheduler

      The Room Scheduler project for Tiny College is a room scheduling application that allows faculty members to reserve rooms for specific dates based on their seating requirements. The application features a user-friendly GUI interface and utilizes a Derby database for data storage. The design follows good Object-Oriented Design and Programming principles, with several classes such as Faculty, Room, Date, and Reservations to handle different aspects of the application. The project implements various user commands, including "Add Faculty" for adding faculty members to the database, "Reserve Faculty Date Seats" for assigning rooms to faculty members based on seat availability, "Status Reservations by Date" to display the faculty members with reserved rooms on a specific date, "Status Waiting List" to display the faculty members on the waiting list, "Add Room Seats" to add new rooms, "Add Date" to add new available dates, "Cancel Reservation Faculty Date" to remove a reservation for a specific faculty member and date, "Status Faculty member" to display the reserved rooms and waitlisted dates for a faculty member, and "Drop Room" to remove a room from the application and reassign reservations if possible. The project emphasizes good Object-Oriented (OO) design practices and uses SQL statements and PreparedStatements for efficient database operations.
